Components of Data Communicaton System

Protocol: Set of rules that governs data communications
Message: Information to be communicated
Sender: Device that sends the data message
Receiver: Device that receives the message
Transmission medium: The physical path by which a message travels from sender to receiver

Characteristic

  • Delivery: System must deliver data to correct destination and by the indended device or user
  • Accuracy: System must deliver data accurately
  • Timeliness: System must deliver data in a timely manner
  • Jitter: Variation in the packet arrival time

Data Flow

Path taken by data within a device, network or organization as it moves from the source to its destination
Categories:

  • Simplex - data is unidirectional, one of the two devices on a link can transmit, eg - keyboard
  • Half-duplex - each station can both transmit and receive but not at the same time
  • Full-duplex - both stations can transmit and receive simultaneously

Type of Connection

  • For communicaton to occur, two devices must be connected to the same link at the same time
  • Possible communications:
    • Point-to-point - Dedicated link between two devices. Most of them uses an actual length of wire or cable to connect the two ends, microwave or satellite links are possible
    • Multipoint - More than two specific devices share a single link

Network Topology

Network topology refers to the arrangement of various components within a network. It describes how different systems, nodes, and devices are interconnected and communicate with each other. Understanding network topology is crucial for designing efficient and reliable networks.

Mesh Topology

Definition:
In a Mesh Topology, every device is connected to every other device in the network. This creates a robust and highly redundant network where each node has a direct link to every other node.

Connection Details:

  • Each node is connected to ( n - 1 ) other nodes.
  • For a network with ( n ) nodes, there are ( n(n-1) ) physical links.
  • Communication occurs in a duplex mode, meaning data can be transmitted and received simultaneously.
  • The number of duplex mode links required is ( \frac{n(n-1)}{2} ).
  • Each device in the network has ( n - 1 ) input/output (I/O) ports.

Advantages:

  • Dedicated Links: Each station has its own dedicated link, ensuring no sharing of bandwidth with other stations, which eliminates traffic congestion.
  • Traffic Problems: Since links are dedicated, there are no traffic problems between devices.
  • Privacy and Security: Data travels directly from one device to another without passing through any other device, ensuring privacy and security.
  • Fault Detection and Isolation: Faults can be easily detected and isolated, as each link can be tested independently.

Disadvantages:

  • Cabling and Ports: The sheer amount of cabling and the number of I/O ports required can be substantial, especially as the network grows.
  • Installation Complexity: Installation and reconnection are complex due to the number of connections required.
  • Space Requirements: The large amount of wiring requires significant physical space.
  • Expensive Hardware: The hardware needed to support all the connections can be expensive.

Use Cases:

  • Military and Government Networks: Where reliability and redundancy are paramount.
  • Critical Infrastructure: Such as energy grids or financial networks, where failure is not an option.


Example:
In a network with 5 nodes, a mesh topology would require ( \frac{5(5-1)}{2} = 10 ) duplex links. Each node would have 4 ports to connect to the other 4 nodes.

Star Topology

Definition:
In a Star Topology, all devices are connected to a central hub or switch. The central hub acts as a conduit to transmit data between the devices.

Advantages:

  • Cost-Effective: Less expensive than mesh topology as fewer connections are required.
  • Ease of Installation: Simple to install and configure, making it a popular choice for many networks.
  • Robustness: If one link fails, only the connected device is affected, not the entire network.
  • Fault Detection: Easy to detect and isolate faults since each device is connected individually to the hub.

Disadvantages:

  • Single Point of Failure: The entire network is dependent on the central hub. If the hub fails, the whole network goes down.
  • Cabling: More cabling is required compared to other topologies like bus or ring.

Use Cases:

  • Office Networks: Common in small to medium-sized office networks where ease of management is important.
  • Home Networks: Frequently used in home networking due to its simplicity and ease of use.

Example:
A star topology is used in many home Wi-Fi networks where all devices (like laptops, smartphones, and printers) connect to a central router.

Bus Topology

Definition:
Bus Topology uses a single backbone cable to which all network devices are connected via drop lines and taps. The backbone cable acts as a shared communication medium that devices use to send data.

Advantages:

  • Ease of Installation: Simple to install, as it requires only a single backbone cable.
  • Reduced Cabling: Uses less cabling compared to mesh and star topologies.

Disadvantages:

  • Reconnection Issues: Reconnecting devices or isolating faults can be difficult and may require the entire network to be taken offline.
  • Fault Isolation: Faults are harder to isolate since all devices share the same communication medium.
  • Limited Scalability: Adding new devices can be challenging, especially if the backbone cable becomes overloaded.

Use Cases:

  • Small Networks: Ideal for small, temporary, or cost-sensitive networks.
  • LANs in Early Networks: Historically used in local area networks (LANs) in small offices or buildings.


Example:
An early Ethernet network used a bus topology where all computers were connected to a single coaxial cable. Each computer would listen for signals on the bus and respond accordingly.

Ring Topology

Definition:
In a Ring Topology, each device is connected to exactly two other devices, forming a circular data path. Data travels in one direction around the ring until it reaches its destination.

Advantages:

  • Ease of Installation: Simple to install and configure as devices are connected in a circular manner.
  • Simplified Fault Identification: Faults are easier to identify because the failure of one device impacts the entire network.
  • Efficient Data Transfer: As data travels in one direction, it avoids collision issues.

Disadvantages:

  • Unidirectional Traffic: Data can only travel in one direction, which can lead to delays.
  • Single Point of Failure: A break in the ring can disable the entire network.

Use Cases:

  • Metropolitan Area Networks (MANs): Often used in MANs where large geographic areas are covered.
  • Token Ring Networks: Historically used in networks like IBMâ€™s Token Ring.


Example:
A token ring network where each computer is connected in a circular layout. A token circulates around the ring, and the computer with the token can send data, ensuring no collision.

Categories of Networks

  • Local Area Networks(LANs)
    • Short distances (area < 2 miles)
    • Designed to provide local interconnectivity
  • Wide Area Netowkrs(WANs)
    • Long distances (Can be worldwide)
    • Provide connectivity over large areas
  • Metropolitan Area Networks(MANs)
    • Provide connectivity over areas such as a city, campus
    • Span 10s of miles

Local Area Network

  • Usually privately owned and connects some hosts in a single office, building, or campus
  • Depending on the needs of an organization, a LAN can be as simple as two PCs and a printer in someone's home office, or it can extend throughout a company and include audio and video devices

Metropolitan Area Network

  • Serves a large geographical area between 5 to 50 kms in range
  • Geographical area can include several buildings, such as a college campus, refferred to as a campus network, or an area as large as a city

Wide Area Network

  • Is also an interconnection of devices capable of communication
    • Differences between a LAN and a WAN :
      • LAN - normally limited in size , spanning an office, a building, or a campus; a WAN has a wider geographical span, spanning a town, a state, a country or even the world
      • LAN interconnects hosts; WAN interconnects connecting devices such as switches, routers or modems
